## INTRO COPY

Most small businesses spend more on IT procurement than they realize — and make more mistakes than they need to. Hardware gets bought at the wrong time (too late, when a machine is already failing, or too early, before it's actually needed). Software licenses get purchased at the wrong tier and then can't be downgraded when nobody's using three-quarters of the features. Vendor contracts get signed without reading the auto-renewal clauses and then cost twice as much when the contract rolls over.

IT procurement isn't exciting, but getting it wrong has real costs: unexpected capital expenses, features you're paying for but not using, and vendor relationships that are hard to exit when you realize you bought the wrong solution.

This guide covers what Northeast Ohio small businesses need to know before buying technology — how to assess what's actually needed, what questions to ask vendors, and how to avoid the most common mistakes.

---

## SECTION 1: Why IT Procurement Goes Wrong

The core problem is that most small business decision-makers are not technology buyers by training. They run a dental practice, a manufacturing shop, an accounting firm. They became experts in their field, not in server specifications or software licensing models.

That knowledge gap creates several predictable failure modes:

**Buying on price instead of value** — The cheapest option often costs more over time. A consumer-grade router at $150 might work fine for a year, then fail in a way that takes down your office for a day while you scramble for a replacement. A business-class router at $400 that lasts five years is the better buy. But when you're looking at the price tag in the moment, you don't see the total cost of ownership.

**No planning for future needs** — Buying technology to solve today's problem without thinking about where your business is going. You buy a server that works for your current team of eight, but doesn't have the capacity or expandability to handle your growth plan over the next three years. Now you're buying again sooner than you should have.

**Underspending on security-critical items** — You don't need the most expensive endpoint protection on the market, but you do need business-class security tools, not consumer-grade ones. A $50 consumer router from the big box store doesn't have the security features, the traffic management capabilities, or the reliability of a business-class device. The risk is asymmetric: saving $200 on a router could mean losing significantly more in a security incident.

**Not factoring in the true cost of implementation** — Hardware has a purchase price, but there are also installation costs, configuration time, migration labor, and training. Software has a license fee, but also implementation, data migration, and the productivity cost as your team learns a new system. Always ask what the total cost of ownership looks like before buying.

**Accepting default vendor settings** — Software vendors want you on the highest-tier plan. Hardware vendors want you to buy extended warranties you don't need. Office 365 plans have feature tiers that most small businesses pay for but never use. Before signing, know exactly what you're paying for and whether you actually need it.

## SECTION 2: The IT Procurement Process for Small Business

A good IT procurement process has five steps before you ever sign a purchase order:

**Step 1: Identify the actual problem**
Don't start by looking at products — start by understanding what you're trying to solve. Is a machine running slowly because it's old, or because it has too many applications loading at startup? Is your current software not meeting your needs, or is your team just not trained on the features you already have? Buying something to solve a problem you haven't fully diagnosed leads to expensive wrong turns.

**Step 2: Define what success looks like**
What does the solution need to do? By when? Who needs to be able to use it? What happens if we don't solve this? Getting specific on requirements before you look at options prevents scope creep and keeps you from buying features you'll never use.

**Step 3: Evaluate total cost of ownership, not just price**
For any significant purchase, get a three-year cost projection. Include the hardware or software cost, implementation and setup, ongoing maintenance and support, and the productivity cost as your team learns the new system. Compare alternatives on that basis, not just the purchase price.

**Step 4: Check vendor reputation and exit terms**
Before buying from a vendor, look at what happens when you want to leave. What does the contract say about data portability? Are there auto-renewal clauses? Is there a buyout fee? Are there competitors that would make switching practical? A vendor that makes it easy to leave is one you can trust to be fair while you're a customer.

**Step 5: Plan the implementation before you buy**
The worst time to discover that a migration is more complicated than expected is after you've already paid for the software. Before you buy, understand what the implementation looks like, how long it takes, what could go wrong, and who is responsible for what. Get this in writing.

## SECTION 3: Common IT Purchases and What to Know Before Buying

**New computers/laptops**
The most common IT purchase. Get the specifications right the first time — an underpowered machine will frustrate your team for years. For a typical knowledge worker: minimum 16GB RAM, 512GB SSD, current-generation processor (Intel i5/i7 or AMD equivalent or Apple M-series). Buy for three years of expected use, not just today's needs. If your team runs browser-heavy workflows, Excel with large datasets, or design software, spec higher than the minimum.

For manufacturing workstations: consult with your IT provider, because some production floor applications have specific hardware requirements or compatibility constraints that rule out certain machines.

**Servers (on-premise or cloud)**
On-premise servers make sense for some businesses but not others. Cloud servers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) offer more flexibility and scale, but require more technical knowledge to manage securely. A hosted server from a managed IT provider can be the right middle ground — you get the control of a dedicated machine without the hardware management burden. Evaluate based on your actual compute needs and how much technical management capacity you have.

**Microsoft 365 and productivity software**
Microsoft 365 Business Basic covers email, Teams, and web versions of Office for $6/user/month. Business Standard adds desktop Office apps for $12/user/month. Business Premium includes security and device management tools. Most small businesses pay for Business Standard and use only a fraction of what they're entitled to. Before adding licenses, audit who actually needs the desktop apps and whether your current tier makes sense.

**Network equipment (routers, switches, access points)**
Business-class equipment from Ubiquiti, Cisco, Netgear ProSAFE, or HP Aruba has features consumer equipment doesn't: VLAN support, Quality of Service controls, central management, stronger security update support. For anything beyond a very small office (5-10 people on a single router), budget for business-class networking equipment.

**Security tools (antivirus, backup, email filtering)**
Buy business-class tools, not consumer ones. Consumer-grade antivirus and consumer-grade backup solutions don't have the management capabilities, the alerting, or the support response that a business needs. A $50/year consumer AV license might miss a threat that a $150/year business endpoint protection product catches.

## SECTION 4: What to Negotiate in IT Vendor Contracts

Small businesses often accept vendor contracts as written, even though there's usually room to negotiate — especially for multi-year commitments or larger purchases.

**Auto-renewal terms** — Many vendors set contracts to auto-renew at a higher price. Negotiate a cap on renewal increases (e.g., no more than 5% increase at renewal). Or negotiate a month-to-month renewal option after the initial term.

**Termination rights** — Can you exit the contract if the vendor is acquired, changes service materially, or fails to meet performance standards? These 'termination for cause' rights are often negotiable, especially for larger purchases.

**Data portability** — Your data should be yours. Negotiate a clause that says the vendor will provide your data in a standard, usable format within 30 days of termination, at no additional cost.

**Service level guarantees** — For managed services, SaaS tools, or cloud infrastructure, negotiate service level commitments with clear remedies if they're not met. If a vendor won't put performance commitments in writing, that's a signal about how they operate.

**Price lock or multi-year discount** — If you're committing to a multi-year contract, ask for a price lock or a discount on the multi-year term. Vendors will often discount 10-20% in exchange for a two-year commitment.

## SECTION 5: How to Know If You're Overpaying

A few signals that you might be overpaying for IT:

**You're on the same plan you signed up for three years ago** — Features, competitive landscape, and your needs change. Annual vendor reviews can catch cases where you're paying for features you don't use or are on a tier below what you actually need.

**You don't have a comparison point** — Check competitors' pricing every few years. IT markets change. A hosting provider that was competitive in 2021 might be 40% more expensive than alternatives in 2024.

**You're paying for per-seat licenses but have users who left** — License auditing (figuring out which seats are actually in use) is something most small businesses skip. Running this audit annually can catch significant waste.

**You're paying for hardware maintenance on devices that would cost less to replace** — Extended warranties on equipment that costs less than $500 to replace are almost never worth it.

**You're paying for a service tier you don't need** — If you're paying for 24/7 support but your team works 8-5 Monday through Friday, you might be able to downgrade to standard business-hours support and save 20-40%.
