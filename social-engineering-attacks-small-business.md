## INTRO COPY

Most data breaches don't start with sophisticated hacking tools. They start with a phone call your receptionist took, an email your accountant almost clicked, or a USB drive someone plugged into a laptop at a trade show. Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into giving up information or access — and it's the primary way attackers breach small businesses.

This guide explains the most common social engineering tactics targeting Ohio small businesses, the real examples we've seen, and what your team actually needs to understand to stop them.

---

## SECTION 1: What Social Engineering Actually Is

Social engineering bypasses your technical security by exploiting human psychology. Instead of hacking your firewall, an attacker calls your office pretending to be IT support and asks for the password to 'run a quick update.' Instead of breaking into your network, they send an email that looks like it's from your bank and wait for someone to enter their credentials.

The attack doesn't target your technology — it targets your people. And your people are generally trusting, helpful, and busy. That's exactly what attackers count on.

Social engineering works because it creates urgency, authority, or fear — the three emotional triggers that make people act before they think. A good social engineer spends time researching their target so the approach feels completely normal.

## SECTION 2: The Most Common Social Engineering Tactics

**Pretexting** — Creating a fake scenario to extract information. An attacker calls pretending to be from your bank, your vendor, or even a law enforcement officer. They have enough information about you that the call feels real. The goal is to get you to verify your identity or provide account details.

**Baiting** — Offering something enticing to get someone to take an action. The classic example is a USB drive left in a company parking lot with a label like 'Payroll Q1 2024.' Someone plugs it into a computer to see what's on it, and that installs malware. More modern versions include fake download links, free gift card offers, and fake job posting sites.

**Quid Pro Quo** — Offering a service in exchange for information. A hacker calls pretending to be from IT support and says they need your password to 'fix a critical issue.' Or they offer a free IT audit and use the intake conversation to learn your network setup, software versions, and admin credentials.

**Tailgating** — Physically following an employee into a secure area. If an attacker looks professional and carries a convincing ID badge, most people will hold the door for them. Once inside, they can plug in a device, access a workstation, or photograph information on whiteboards.

**Phishing combined with pretexting** — The most common combination. An email that looks exactly like a legitimate message from Microsoft, your bank, or a vendor you use. The message creates urgency — your password expires today — and includes a link to a fake login page. Your team member enters their credentials and the attacker has them.

## SECTION 3: The Human Triggers Social Engineers Exploit

Understanding how these attacks work is important. Understanding why they work is more important — because that's what lets you build defenses that actually hold.

**Authority** — People defer to experts. An attacker who sounds like an IT professional, a bank representative, or a law enforcement officer will get information that a stranger with no credentials wouldn't. This is why 'I need to speak to whoever handles IT' is such a valuable piece of information for an attacker doing research.

**Urgency** — 'This needs to be done right now.' Attackers create artificial deadlines that prevent your team from taking time to verify. If someone feels they need to act immediately, they skip steps they'd normally take.

**Reciprocity** — 'I helped you, now you owe me.' An attacker who does something helpful first — 'I fixed that printer issue for you last week' — creates a felt obligation to return the favor. The next call might be 'I need your VPN credentials to finish the remote session we started.'

**Social proof** — 'Your colleague already approved this.' Attackers reference real people inside the organization to make their request feel validated. 'Sarah asked me to send you the updated vendor contract — can you confirm your email so I can send it?'

**Liking and familiarity** — People do business with people they like. An attacker who takes time to build a relationship — commenting on your company blog, connecting on LinkedIn, emailing with a friendly tone — gets more trust than a cold stranger.

**Scarcity** — 'This offer expires in 24 hours.' FOMO drives action. An attacker offering a limited-time deal, a time-sensitive opportunity, or a one-time exception gets compliance that wouldn't happen under normal circumstances.

## SECTION 4: Real Social Engineering Scenarios That Hit Ohio Businesses

**The Fake IT Call** — A manufacturing company in the Akron area got a call from someone claiming to be from their managed IT provider's help desk. The caller knew the names of several employees, the name of their office manager, and the fact that they'd recently switched vendors. They asked for VPN credentials 'to resolve a connectivity issue.' The office manager almost gave them out — but checked with their actual IT provider first. It was a scam.

**The Vendor Email Update** — A Canton accounting firm received an email from what appeared to be one of their vendors, with an updated bank account for future payments. The email looked completely legitimate — same formatting, same sender name, similar email address. The firm updated the payment details and the next invoice payment went to the attacker's account. They didn't catch it for six weeks.

**The USB Drive** — A local nonprofit attended a community event. Someone had left branded USB drives in the welcome bag. One staff member plugged one into their laptop 'to see what was on it.' The malware on the USB immediately spread to their network. It took NHM three days to fully clean up the infection.

**The LinkedIn Connection** — A Youngstown business owner connected with what appeared to be a recruiter on LinkedIn. They exchanged several messages, and the 'recruiter' sent a PDF resume. The PDF contained a malicious macro that gave the attacker access to the business owner's email. They used that access to send wire transfer requests to the company's finance team.

## SECTION 5: How to Protect Your Team

The best social engineering defense is a well-trained team that knows what to look for and feels empowered to slow down and verify.

**Train your team on recognition** — Every employee who handles money, email, or sensitive data needs regular training on social engineering tactics. Use real examples from your industry. Make it practical, not theoretical.

**Build a verification culture** — Teach your team to verify unusual requests through a different channel. If someone calls asking for credentials, tell them to hang up and call the company's official IT number. If an email asks for a wire transfer, call the requestor directly using a known number.

**Use multi-factor authentication** — Even if an attacker gets a password through a phishing call or email, MFA stops them from accessing the account. This is one of the highest-ROI security controls available.

**Establish clear procedures for sensitive requests** — Wire transfers, password changes, and access provisioning should follow a documented process that includes verification steps. No one should ever be asked to bypass these steps.

**Run simulated attacks** — NHM runs phishing simulation campaigns against client teams so you can see who would click, who would respond to a vishing call, and who would give up credentials. You use the results to train the people who need it most.

**Limit what information is public** — Review what your company shares on LinkedIn, your website, and social media. An attacker doing research needs to know enough about your business to sound credible. The less public information available, the harder their job is.

**Document incidents and share lessons learned** — When a social engineering attempt is caught, use it as a training opportunity for the whole team. Real examples from your own organization are far more memorable than generic case studies.
