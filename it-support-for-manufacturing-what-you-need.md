## INTRO COPY

If you run a manufacturing shop — a machine shop, a fabrication business, a parts manufacturer — you already know that production schedules don't wait for IT problems. When a CNC machine goes offline because of a network issue, every hour of downtime costs real money: labor sitting idle, jobs falling behind, penalties in your contracts.

Most general-purpose IT support doesn't understand that reality. They schedule maintenance windows during business hours, treat 'the floor' as just another office, and don't know the difference between an OT network and a standard business LAN. When something goes wrong on the production floor, they make it worse by not understanding how the equipment connects.

This guide covers what manufacturing IT support actually looks like — the specific requirements, the mistakes businesses make, and what to look for in a provider that understands production environments.

---

## SECTION 1: Why Manufacturing IT Is Different

Manufacturing environments have characteristics that most IT providers don't encounter in a standard office:

**Operational technology (OT) networks** — Your CNC machines, PLCs, SCADA systems, and industrial robots all have network connections. These devices are often designed for reliability, not security. Many run old operating systems that can't be patched, have default credentials that were never changed, and connect to the same network as your office computers without any segmentation.

**Production-first uptime requirements** — An hour of downtime on the floor has a direct, quantifiable cost. A managed IT provider that treats a four-hour response window as acceptable is telling you they don't understand your environment. Manufacturing IT needs response times that match production reality.

**Shift-based support** — Your team works in shifts. Your IT support should be available when they work. If someone's second shift runs into a problem at 9pm and your IT provider's help desk is only staffed until 5pm, that problem becomes an 8am issue — and now you've lost an entire shift.

**Vendor coordination complexity** — Manufacturing equipment vendors often have remote access agreements that let them dial into your equipment to run diagnostics or push updates. Managing those vendor relationships — who has access, when they can access it, what they're allowed to do — is a real IT discipline that most shops don't have a handle on.

**Traceability and documentation requirements** — If you're under any regulatory requirements — ISO 9001, ITAR, FDA quality system regulations — your IT infrastructure needs documentation, audit trails, and change management. This isn't optional in manufacturing.

## SECTION 2: The IT Problems Manufacturing Businesses Actually Face

These are the issues NHM LLC sees repeatedly when working with Northeast Ohio manufacturing operations:

**Flat networks with no segmentation** — The office computers, the production floor devices, and the vendor remote access all live on the same network. One compromised device spreads to everything. Without network segmentation, a virus on an engineer laptop can reach the machine that runs your best profitable part.

**Unpatched and unmonitored equipment** — Those CNC machines probably run Windows 7 or an embedded OS that the manufacturer hasn't updated in years. Traditional IT providers won't touch them because patching might break the machine's certification. But without monitoring, you have no visibility into whether something is already compromised.

**Vendor remote access with no controls** — Your machine tool vendor can log in to run diagnostics. That's useful. But when was the last time someone checked what they actually accessed, what they changed, or whether their own security is up to your standards? In most shops, that access was set up once and never reviewed.

**No documented IT procedures** — When the IT person who set everything up leaves, the knowledge leaves with them. Every shop with more than a few employees needs documented procedures: what to do when a machine goes offline, who to call, what the escalation path looks like.

**Inadequate backup and recovery** — If a machine's control PC fails, how long does it take to restore? If your ERP server goes down, do you have the data? Most small manufacturers have gaps in their backup and recovery that would be catastrophic if something actually failed.

## SECTION 3: What Real Manufacturing IT Support Includes

The IT provider you want is one that has worked with manufacturing environments and understands the floor as well as the office. Here's what that looks like:

**Network architecture that separates OT and IT** — Your production floor network and your office network should be segmented. Traffic from the floor shouldn't be able to reach office systems directly. This requires switch configuration, VLAN setup, and firewall policies that a generalist might not know how to design.

**Monitoring that respects OT constraints** — You can monitor network traffic and device behavior without patching the machine itself. Network-level monitoring catches anomalies — unusual traffic patterns, devices communicating with suspicious external addresses, consumption spikes — that suggest a compromise before it becomes a shutdown.

**Vendor access management** — Who has remote access to your equipment? What are they allowed to do? When do they connect? These questions need answers, and the answers need to be enforced technically, not just by policy.

**Backup and recovery for OT systems** — Control PC images, ERP data, production schedules — these need to be backed up and recoverable. You need to know exactly how long it takes to restore each system and what that restoration process looks like.

**Response times that match production** — When the floor goes down, you need someone who understands the environment, can triage accurately over the phone, and can get on-site fast when remote support isn't enough.

**Documentation and compliance support** — If you need to pass an ISO audit, your IT infrastructure needs to support that. That means change logs, access records, network diagrams, and configuration documentation that most shops don't have.

## SECTION 4: Questions to Ask Any IT Provider Before Hiring Them

Before you sign with any managed IT provider, ask these questions — and pay attention to how they answer:

Q: Have you worked with manufacturing environments before?
If they say 'we support all industries,' that's a red flag. Manufacturing has specific requirements. You want a provider with documented manufacturing clients.

Q: What's your response time for a production floor emergency?
If they can't give you a specific SLA that matches your shift schedule, keep looking.

Q: How do you handle devices that can't be patched?
If they say 'we update everything on a regular schedule,' they don't understand OT. Patching some equipment will void the manufacturer's certification or break the machine. You need a provider who knows how to handle this.

Q: Can you manage vendor remote access?
The answer should include technical controls — not just a policy document that says 'we trust them.'

Q: Do you have experience with ISO/ITAR/FDA compliance requirements?
If you're in a regulated manufacturing environment, this isn't optional.

Q: What does your documentation include?
Network diagrams, device inventories, configuration records, and change logs should all be included. If they can't produce a network diagram for their own environment, they're not going to produce one for yours.
